Hawksbill Sea Turtle

Eretmochelys imbricata
IUCN Red List Status: CRITICALLY ENDANGERED (CR)

Habitat & Range

Hawksbills are found primarily in the tropical reefs of the Indian, Pacific, and Atlantic Oceans. They are heavily associated with coral reefs, rocky areas, and shallow coastal areas where their primary food source—sea sponges—is abundant.

Primary Threats

The historical decline of the Hawksbill was largely driven by the illegal wildlife trade. Their beautifully patterned shells were the primary source of commercial "tortoiseshell" used for jewelry and ornaments. Today, they face extreme threats from accidental entanglement in fishing gear (bycatch), loss of coral reef habitats due to climate change, and the illegal harvesting of their eggs from nesting beaches.

Population Estimates

Due to their highly migratory nature, an exact population count is difficult, but it is estimated there are only 20,000 to 23,000 nesting females worldwide. Their population is believed to have declined by 80% over the last three turtle generations.
